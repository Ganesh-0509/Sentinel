# Factories Act 1948 — hazardous processes (summary)

## Occupier's duty

The occupier of a factory carries a general duty to ensure, so far as is reasonably
practicable, the health, safety and welfare of all workers while they are at work. This
covers plant and systems of work that are safe and without risk to health, arrangements
for safe handling and storage of substances, adequate instruction, training and
supervision, and a safe working environment.

## Hazardous processes — additional obligations

Where a factory carries on a process notified as hazardous, additional obligations apply:

- **Disclosure of information.** The occupier must disclose information about the hazards
  involved, and the measures taken to control them, to workers, to the local authority and
  to the general public in the vicinity.
- **Site appraisal.** A prior appraisal of the health and safety aspects of the site may be
  required before the process is permitted.
- **Health records and medical examination.** Workers exposed to hazardous substances are
  subject to medical supervision and record-keeping.
- **Permissible exposure limits.** Exposure of workers to hazardous chemical substances
  must not exceed the prescribed limits.
- **Emergency standards.** Where the Central Government considers existing provisions
  inadequate for a given hazardous process, emergency standards may be laid down and are
  enforceable as if part of the rules.

## Safety committee and safety officers

Factories carrying on hazardous processes are required to constitute a safety committee
with representation from both workers and management, to promote co-operation on safety
and health. Factories above prescribed size or risk must employ qualified safety officers.

## Worker rights and duties

Workers have a right to be warned about imminent danger. Where workers have reasonable
apprehension of a likelihood of imminent danger to their life or health, they may bring it
to the notice of the occupier and the inspector; the occupier is required to take remedial
action. Workers also carry duties to use safety appliances provided and not to wilfully
interfere with safety measures.

## Emergency preparedness and reporting

Factories handling hazardous substances must maintain an on-site emergency plan and
detailed disaster control measures, make workers aware of them, and rehearse them.
Accidents and dangerous occurrences must be notified to the prescribed authority, and
records maintained. Where an accident causes death or serious bodily injury, additional
reporting and investigation obligations follow.

## Relevance to compound risk monitoring

Two themes bear directly on an intelligence layer of this kind:

1. The duty is **anticipatory** — controlling risk so far as reasonably practicable, not
   merely reacting after an event. Evidence that a developing hazard was detectable but not
   acted upon is material.
2. The duty includes **warning workers of imminent danger**, which implies the information
   must actually reach the people exposed, in time to act.
